There's a question I ask myself every time I'm about to make a design decision: **could I explain this to someone without making their head hurt?**

It's not a rhetorical question. It's a working tool.

## The code you write today will be read by someone tomorrow

And that someone, more often than not, is you. At three in the morning. With a production incident. With no context.

When you truly internalize that, architecture stops being an intellectual exercise and becomes an act of responsibility. Every layer you add unnecessarily, every premature abstraction, every "we'll refactor it later" is a debt that someone — you, your team, a new hire — will have to pay back with interest.

Well-designed architecture is the most honest documentation that exists. It doesn't lie. It doesn't go stale. It's right there, in the project structure, telling you how the system was conceived and why.

## Teaching and designing are the same skill

It took me years to realize this. I thought architecture was a pure engineering problem, and that teaching was something separate — something you did when giving a talk or a mentorship session.

I was wrong.

When you have to teach something to someone, the first obstacle is always the same: if you don't understand the concept well enough, you can't explain it. Teaching forces you to identify the gaps in your own understanding.

The same is true for design. If you can't justify why you used a repository instead of a service, why you separated the domain from the infrastructure, why that module doesn't depend on that other one, then there's something you didn't fully understand. Or something you're doing out of inertia, because you saw it in a tutorial, because "it's what everyone uses."

**Designing well forces you to understand well. Teaching well forces you to understand well. That's not a coincidence.**

## Decisions change when you think about communicating them

Some time ago, on a mid-scale project, we had to decide how to handle business logic. The quick option was to put everything in controllers — something that technically worked and that the team already knew.

I asked myself: "How would I explain this to someone new joining the project in three months?"

The answer was uncomfortable: "I'd have to walk them through each controller separately, because each one has its own special logic, and none of it is in a predictable place."

That was enough. Not for elegance, not to follow a trendy pattern, but because I recognized that structure would make onboarding — and maintenance — needlessly hard.

We moved the logic to explicit use cases. Three months later, a new person joined the team and was contributing within two days. That doesn't happen by accident.

## The problem with "clever" architectures

There's an antipattern I see constantly: architectures designed to impress rather than to communicate.

Layers upon layers. Abstractions abstracting abstractions. Patterns applied because the architect learned them in a book, not because the problem needed them.

The result is a system no one can read without a twenty-minute guide, that has bugs taking days to find because the logic is spread across five different responsibilities, and that grows fear around touching code because "who knows what breaks."

That's not architecture. That's ego disguised as technique.

The architecture that teaches is the one that says "look, here a request comes in, here it gets validated, here the business logic runs, here it gets persisted." Linear. Predictable. Boring in the best sense of the word.

## What changed in how I make decisions

Today, before choosing a pattern or a structure, I ask myself three questions:

1. **Can I explain this decision in two sentences?** If I can't, it's probably too complex or I don't understand it well enough.
2. **Could a dev with six months of experience navigate this?** I'm not aiming for the lowest common denominator — I'm aiming for clarity.
3. **Does this decision close doors or open them?** Good architecture lets you change your mind. Bad architecture traps you.

They're not perfect questions. But they changed the way I think about design.

## Closing

Next time you're facing an architecture decision, before opening Stack Overflow or asking an AI to generate code, ask yourself: **could I explain this to someone?**

If the answer is yes, you're probably on the right track.
If the answer is "it depends on a lot of things you'd need to understand first," there's work to do.

Designing is teaching. Teaching is understanding. And truly understanding is the hardest — and most valuable — thing we can do as professionals.
