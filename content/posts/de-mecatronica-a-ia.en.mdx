When I started studying Mechatronics Engineering, my picture of the future was clear: I was going to design robots. Industrial arms, control systems, actuators, sensors — the physical world controlled by software.

I wasn't wrong. The path just turned out to be longer and wider than I imagined.

## The physical world as foundation

Mechatronics gives you something other specialized engineering fields don't: a systems perspective. When you design a 6-degree-of-freedom robotic arm, you're not thinking about just servos or just kinematics or just code. You're thinking about how everything interacts: the mechanical structure defines movement limits, the electronics define control precision, and the software defines the system's intelligence.

That systems mindset was the foundation for everything that came after.

At university I worked with SolidWorks, finite element analysis, digital manufacturing — 3D printing, laser cutting, design for manufacturing. Every project was an integration exercise: the mechanical design had to be manufacturable, the electronics had to physically fit, the software had to run on a microcontroller with limited resources.

## The unexpected turn

My first job outside pure engineering was at Teleperformance — operations management with teams of up to 1,200 people. Far from robotics, but surprisingly relevant. I learned that the most complex systems aren't technical — they're human.

Managing an operation at that scale is systems engineering applied to people: processes, metrics, optimization, scalability. The same concepts I used to design a control system I applied to designing a workflow that functioned with hundreds of people.

## Where it all converges

When I joined ManageEngine as a Technical Consultant, everything started connecting. ITSM (IT Service Management) is, in essence, a system: tickets come in, get processed, get resolved. The flows have rules, exceptions, performance metrics. It's engineering applied to support operations.

And when they proposed integrating AI into that system, the convergence was complete. The automation project with RAG, Pinecone, and GPT-4 required exactly what my background had given me: understanding the complete system (not just the AI part), designing integration between different components (web scraping, vector databases, APIs, ITSM), and making everything work reliably in production.

## What mechatronics taught me about AI

AI isn't magic. It's another component in a system. Just as a servo needs a control signal, an LLM needs clean data and relevant context. Just as a sensor has noise, a model has hallucinations. Just as a mechanical system has tolerances, an AI system has error margins.

The mechatronics engineer mindset — thinking in complete systems, not isolated components — turned out to be exactly what I needed to make AI work in real-world contexts.

Today I work as a Business Systems & AI Specialist, and the title sounds very far from "Mechatronics Engineer." But every project I do uses that foundation: thinking in systems, integrating components, making complex things work reliably.

The path wasn't straight. But every step connects.
